How To Win A Fight (With Data)

Much has changed since the first punch thrown in Ultimate Fighting Championship 1, 1993.

New rules have been implimented: no more legal punches to the groin. Champions have reigned, and some quickly dethroned. Women, who UFC president Dana White once said would "never fight in the UFC", are now on almost every card. And the UFC has become an international powerhouse in sports and politics having broken Madison Square Garden gate records, and possibly helped elect a US president. There's no denying UFC's influence.

But, what can we glean from nearly 6,489 fights worth of data spanning 2010 to 2024?

Merging datasets on fighter statistics and fight records from hundreds of UFC events in the past 14 years, are there correlations that might help us predict a fight's outcome?



WHICH CORNER WINS MORE?

If you've got red tape on your gloves, you're in luck, because historically in the UFC, 58% of the time, red beats blue.

The higher ranked fighter is often assigned the red corner. Champions defending their title are always red, and challengers are always blue.

So it stands to reason that red would have a higher win percentage when looking at every fight. But, surprisingly, when looking just at fights when both neither fighter is ranked better than the other, the red corner still comes out with a win 58% of the time.



WHAT DECIDES A WINNER?

Statistically, some finishing moves are far more common than others.

The number one way to end a fight is by decision; 38% of all fights end with three judges unanimously choosing the winner.

Punches end fights over 21% of the time.

Of all submissions, the most common win comes from Rear Naked Choke at 7%, followed by Guillotine at 3%, and Arm Bar at just around 2%.




IS HEIGHT AN ADVANTAGE?



Weight is obviously a significant advantage -- it's why weight classes exist. But, there's no height classes, and for good reason.

The stats show a minor correlation between fighter height and win result ... The taller and the shorter fighter win similarly, 52% of the time the taller fighter won in the last 14 years. However, fights where there is a 10 to 20cm difference in height, the taller fighter win rate moves up to 55%.

But, once you get to 20cm or more difference in height between fighters, the small advantage weens. In 27 fights where that criteria is met, the shorter contender wins slightly more often at 52% of the time.



Fighter "reach" mirrors height with 52% of winning fights in favor of the fighter with longer arms.



IS THERE A "BETTER" STANCE?

There's essentially three common fighting stances, the most prevelant is "orthodox", where the fighter's right hand and foot is forward. And the most common matchup is orthodox versus orthodox, at 56%, or 3,628 of 6,489 fights.

The second most popular stance is "southpaw", where the left hand and foot is forward, followed by the rare ambidexterous stance called "switch".



Looking at the stance matchups where different stances face one another, statistically orthodox loses to both southpaw and switch at a slightly higher rate than the reverse. The switch stance is slightly more likely to win against all other stances.




DOES AGE MATTER?

Perhaps the most significant predictor of a fight's outcome is the age gap between fighters. There is a strong correlation between an increasing age gap and the win rate for the younger fighter.



Among all 2,522 fights where the age gap is only 5 or more years, the younger fighter won 60 percent of the time. Just looking at a 5 to 9 year age gap, the younger fighter wins 59% of the time.

Add another 5 years, in 437 fights where the fighters' age gap is 10 or more years, the younger fighter wins 70% of the time.

And with a 15+ year age gap, 27 fights with that difference, the younger fighter wins 78% of bouts.

...



If you're fighting in the UFC, you want to be 5 or more years younger than your opponent, taller (but not too tall), have a longer reach, and fighting with switch stance against an orthodox fighter. And choose the red corner.

The number of times a fighter has matched all these criteria in the last 14 years is only 16 times out of 6489 fights. And they won 88% of the time.
